Design Grammars system supporting decision-making in Construction across platforms and disciplines.

SPEAKER NOTES — ptBIM 2026 (FEUP, 17–19 June 2026)
Friday, 9:20. ~12–15 min talk. Speak the lines below; slides only support.
Principle (per consultation): top-down — hook with the problem, teach the few

2 slide. Roadmap
SPEECH:
Here is where we are going. First a problem — why our handovers keep losing
intent. Then three words we need to share a language: design knowledge, design
rules, design states. Then the gap that no current tool closes, and our
proposal — a system we call Design Grammars. Finally I will show the prototype
working end to end, from a client's brief to a validated design. Don't worry
about the technical terms; I will build them up as we go.

====================================================================
3 slide. Hook
(On screen: RIBA phases — intent stripping away from Concept to Maintenance)
SPEECH:
Let me start with one question: how much of your design intent actually
survives the next handover? Picture a project moving from concept, to
developed design, to construction, to maintenance. At every handover we pass
files — geometry, sheets, models. But the WHY behind them — the rules, the
constraints, the reasoning — stays in our heads, or dies inside a tool. Each
transfer strips a little more intent away. What we would really want is for
that intent to travel with the project. And to travel, it has to be encoded.
Encoded design intent is what we call design knowledge.

====================================================================
4 slide. Design Knowledge — the logic behind the model
SPEECH:
So — design knowledge. Simply: the logic behind the model. It is
machine-readable design intent, made of two things. Design RULES — the
constraints and the shaping logic. And design STATES — the actual solutions
those rules produce. Hold on to these two words, rules and states; the whole
talk hangs on them.

====================================================================
5 slide. Design Rules — morphologic atoms of design knowledge
SPEECH:
First, design rules. A design rule is an explicit, codified constraint that a
design must satisfy — for example, "a hexagonal unit must sit within its level
boundaries." Architects already think in these rules; we just rarely write
them down in a form a machine can act on. Here is one expressed graphically.

====================================================================
6 slide. Design Rules — machine-readable (SWRL)
SPEECH:
And here is the same rule made machine-readable. Take a plain constraint —
"the maximum building height is 75 metres" — and the system turns it into a
small logical statement: if a building is taller than 75, flag it. That formal
form is what lets the computer check the rule for us. You don't need to read
the syntax — the point is that a sentence in natural language became something
executable.

====================================================================
7 slide. The Design State
SPEECH:
The second word: design state. A design state is a uniquely identified
snapshot of a design at one moment — the input parameters that defined it, and
the geometry that resulted. Think of it as a named save-point of your
parametric model: one you can store, return to, and compare.

====================================================================
8 slide. The Design State — and why it matters
SPEECH:
Why does that matter? Because the system both validates each state against the
rules, and can generate new states from them. So design stops being one frozen
file. It becomes a space of solutions you can explore, compare, and reinstate —
all while staying checked against the original intent.

====================================================================
9 slide. The Knowledge Gap
SPEECH:
So where is the gap? BIM manages information safisctically — but design knowledge
stays fragmented across files, platforms, and disciplines. And our parametric
tools — Grasshopper, Dynamo — hold the real logic, yet as opaque node graphs
that die at export. The rules get rebuilt in every tool, and never actually
shared. That is the gap: we exchange the RESULTS of design thinking, but never
the thinking itself.

====================================================================
10 slide. The Design Grammars
SPEECH:
Our answer to that gap is a design-knowledge management system. We call it
Design Grammars.

====================================================================
11 slide. Goal
SPEECH:
Its goal is a single idea: a Shared Knowledge Core that carries encoded intent
across every platform in the project, and reuses it to validate and generate
design states as the project develops. Concretely — a rule like "hexagonal
units must align with level boundaries" is authored once, at concept stage,
and still checks the BIM model at construction stage. Same rule, one source,
travelling with the project.

====================================================================
12 slide. The Three Pillars
SPEECH:
For that to work, the system needs three properties. It must be explicit and
machine-readable. It must be generative — able to produce solutions, not just
store them. And it must be cross-platform — independent of any single tool. One
technology gives us all three: an ontology-driven knowledge graph. Let me
unpack those two words, because most of our field does not use them daily.

====================================================================
13 slide. Knowledge Graph — a structured way to organise data
SPEECH:
First, a knowledge graph. Forget architecture for a second. When Netflix says
"because you watched this, you might like that," it is walking a graph — films,
actors, genres, all joined by meaningful links. "A friend of a friend" on
social media is the same idea. A knowledge graph stores data as things and the
relationships between them, so the data keeps its meaning. And unlike a
spreadsheet or a plain database, it can infer — follow the links to reach new
facts. We use it to hold design rules from many sources and reuse them.

====================================================================
14 slide. Ontology — organised domain knowledge
SPEECH:
Second, ontology. An ontology is simply the agreed vocabulary and rules of a
domain — the grammar of the graph. It names what kinds of things exist —
classes, like building, room, door; how they relate — a door connects two
spaces, a room is part of a building; the properties they carry; and the actual
instances. It is how we make sure that everyone, and every tool, means the same
thing by "room" or "wall."

====================================================================
15 slide. Ontology — generated OntoGraph
SPEECH:
And this is not only theory drawn by hand. Here is an ontology our system
generated automatically from encoded design intent — the design rules
themselves, turned into a structured, queryable web of concepts. Ten seconds on
this: just notice it is organised, and machine-built, not hand-drawn.

====================================================================
16 slide. DG System Framework — 5 layers, Core–Periphery
SPEECH:
Now the system itself, at a glance. It is a core–periphery model with five
layers, from the outside in. Human input enters through a Communication layer;
AI and APIs translate it into structured logic; an Analysis layer evaluates it;
and a Data layer — a graph database — holds it as the shared core. You don't
need the detail now. The takeaway: abstract intent goes in one side, and comes
out the other as live validation against the BIM model.

====================================================================
17 slide. DG System Prototype — Architecture
SPEECH:
Under the hood, this already runs as a working prototype — a web platform, a
local language model that interprets the rules, a graph database for the core,
and a Grasshopper connector to the parametric and BIM tools. Again, ten
seconds: I only want you to see that it is real, integrated, and built on open
components.

====================================================================
18 slide. Use Case — from client requirements to validated design state
SPEECH:
Let me prove it with one walkthrough — a real path from a client's
requirements all the way to a validated design state, in four phases.

====================================================================
19 slide. Phase 1–2. Client Brief and Rule Authoring (Web — Grammar Viewer)
SPEECH:
Phases one and two — the brief and the rules. On the web interface, a planner
writes requirements in plain language: heights, spacing, adjacencies. The
system encodes them into the Knowledge Core. In our tests, a first-time user
with no formal-logic background authored and validated a rule in about fifteen
minutes — against roughly forty-five by hand.

====================================================================
20 slide. Phase 3. Design Bootstrap (Grasshopper Add-in)
SPEECH:
Phase three — the design. Through a Grasshopper add-in, the computational
designer pulls those same rules into the parametric model, binds them to
geometry, and generates options. Because the rules live in the core, a single
edit re-evaluates every alternative in seconds — so instead of three to five
options per session, we explored twelve to twenty.

====================================================================
21 slide. Phase 4. Validation and Communication (Web — Design State Viewer)
SPEECH:
Phase four — validation and communication. Every design state is checked
against the rules and published back to the platform: colour-coded, with a full
history you can query by rule or by state, reinstate, and compare. And it is
transparent — no black box. You always see which rule passed or failed, and
why.

====================================================================
22 slide. Conclusion
SPEECH:
To sum up: we have a working prototype that encodes, manages, and reuses design
knowledge across the lifecycle. It already ingests rules from multiple sources,
validates evolving design states, and records them so a parametric model can be
reinstated. The honest limits — our rule coverage is still a subset of full
logic, and we have evaluated it only in academic case studies; production
stress-testing is next. But the core idea holds: when intent travels as shared
knowledge instead of detached files, almost none of it is lost at the handover.
